Carl Edward Sagan (November 9, 1934 – December 20, 1996) was an American astronomer, planetary scientist, cosmologist, astrophysicist, astrobiologist, author, and science communicator. His best known scientific contribution is research on extraterrestrial life, including experimental demonstration of the production of amino acids from basic chemicals by radiation.

Work

  • 1960 - Doctorate in astronomy and astrophysics.
  • 1960 - 1962 - Was a fellow in astronomy at the University of California
  • 1962 - 2968 - Worked at Harvard University and the Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
  • 1968 - Became the director of Cornell University’s Laboratory for Planetary Studies
  • 1971 - Became a full professor at the Cornell University’s Laboratory for Planetary Studies

Books

  • 1973 - The Cosmic Connection: An Extraterrestrial Perspective (with Jerome Agel)
  • 1980 - Cosmos (with his wife, Ann Druyan)
  • 1985 - Contact
  • 1994 - Pale Blue Dot: A Vision of the Human Future in Space

Awards

  • The Pulitzer Prize for nonfition (1978)
  • The NASA's Distinguished Public Service Medal (1977 and 1981)
  • The Ørsted Medal, from the American Association of Physics Teachers (1990)
  • The Public Welfare Medal, awarded by the National Academy of Sciences (1994)
